Qiraja (Lease)

A property rental or lease agreement in Kosovo

Qiraja is the general term for a lease or rental agreement in Kosovo, by which a landlord (qiradhënës) grants a tenant (qiramarrës) the right to use a property for a defined period in exchange for periodic rent (qiraja/qiraja mujore). Kosovo's lease law is primarily contained in the Law on Obligational Relations.

A residential lease for a term of more than one year should be in writing. Although notarisation is not mandated for ordinary residential leases, a notarised lease agreement creates an enforcement title that allows the landlord to proceed directly to enforcement (without a separate court judgment) in the event of non-payment or failure to vacate.

How It Works in Practice

Lease agreements in Kosovo typically specify the monthly rent, lease term, security deposit (kaution), permitted uses of the property, maintenance responsibilities, and notice periods for termination. Security deposits are commonly one to three months' rent and must be returned at the end of the tenancy if no damage beyond normal wear has occurred.

Commercial lease agreements are more complex and typically negotiated between the parties without statutory minimum protections. Key commercial lease terms include rent review mechanisms, assignment and subletting rights, fit-out obligations, and early termination penalties. Buyers purchasing commercial property with existing tenants should review all leases before completing the acquisition.

Key Facts

Governing LawLaw on Obligational Relations (Kosovo)
Written FormRecommended for all leases; required for terms over one year
NotarisationOptional but creates enforcement title
DepositKaution — typically one to three months' rent
Tenant ProtectionsLimited; terms largely by agreement

Kosovo does not have a comprehensive residential tenancy act with strong tenant protections equivalent to those in Western European countries. Landlords and tenants are largely free to agree their own terms within the framework of the Law on Obligational Relations. Tenants should carefully read and negotiate lease terms before signing.

Common Mistake: Tenants often pay a deposit without obtaining a written receipt or including deposit terms in the lease agreement. Without documentary evidence, recovering a deposit at the end of a tenancy can be very difficult.
Expert Tip: Include a detailed inventory (inventari) signed by both parties at the start of the tenancy, recording the condition of the property and all fixtures. This protects both landlord and tenant when the deposit is settled at the end.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a Kosovo residential lease need to be notarised?

Notarisation is not legally required but is highly advisable as it creates an enforcement title, allowing the landlord to proceed to enforcement without a separate court judgment.

What is a kaution in a Kosovo lease?

It is the security deposit paid by the tenant at the start of a tenancy, typically one to three months' rent, refundable at the end if there is no damage beyond normal wear.

Are there strong tenant protection laws in Kosovo?

Kosovo does not have a comprehensive residential tenancy act. Protections are limited and largely depend on what the parties agree in the lease contract.
