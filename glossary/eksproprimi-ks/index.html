Eksproprimi (Expropriation)

The compulsory acquisition of private property by the Kosovo state for public purposes

Eksproprimi is the compulsory acquisition of private real property by the Kosovo state or a municipal authority for a public purpose — such as road construction, infrastructure development, or public utility projects. Kosovo's Law on Expropriation (Law No. 03/L-205) governs the process and guarantees the right to fair compensation.

Expropriation can only occur for a legitimate public purpose (qëllim publik) and only after the expropriating authority has made genuine efforts to negotiate a voluntary purchase with the property owner. The state must pay fair market value compensation before or simultaneously with taking possession of the property.

How It Works in Practice

Buyers should check whether a property is in an area designated for future public infrastructure — roads, railways, utilities — before purchasing. Kosovo's municipal spatial plans (planet komunale) and national development plans identify areas subject to potential expropriation. Planning offices and the relevant spatial plan documents are the primary sources.

If a property owner disagrees with the amount of compensation offered, they have the right to challenge the valuation before the Kosovo Property Agency and, if necessary, the courts. Independent expert valuations are commonly commissioned in such disputes.

Expropriation is relatively rare in Kosovo but has occurred in connection with major infrastructure projects, including highway construction and urban regeneration initiatives. Properties in the path of planned infrastructure routes are at particular risk, and this should be investigated during due diligence.

Common Mistake: Buyers sometimes purchase properties in infrastructure corridors without checking the municipal spatial plan. A property designated for road widening or public utility works can be subject to expropriation at market value — a risk that must be priced into any purchase.
Expert Tip: Ask the municipal urban planning office for a certificate (certifikatë) confirming the property is not designated for expropriation or infrastructure development before signing the purchase contract. Some offices issue this document routinely as part of the planning permission process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the Kosovo state expropriate private property?

Yes, but only for a legitimate public purpose and after paying fair market value compensation to the owner. The process is governed by Law No. 03/L-205.

How can I check if a property is at risk of expropriation?

Review the municipal spatial plan (plani komunal) and national infrastructure development plans at the municipal urban planning office, or request a planning certificate confirming the property's status.

What compensation is payable on expropriation?

The law requires payment of fair market value, assessed at the time of expropriation. Owners can challenge the assessed value through the Kosovo Property Agency or the courts.
